## Post

VCs used to fund the team that could build it.

Now everyone can build it. AI took that advantage off the table.

So what are investors actually looking for in 2026?

---

I've been paying close attention to how the fundraising conversation has shifted. The phrase I keep hearing from investors: "high-context founders."

What that means in practice: they want founders who already know the customer cold before the first dollar is raised. Not "I've done the research." More like: "I spent 10 years in this industry, I know exactly where the pain is, and I already know the first 100 people who will pay for this."

Distribution advantage is the new moat. Not the tech.

---

The math has changed. AI coding tools are genuinely democratizing the ability to build software. A 2-person team can ship what used to take 20 engineers. That's real.

But it also means the barrier to "build it" has collapsed for everyone. Your competitors have access to the same tools. If your pitch is "we move fast and ship great software" — so does every other founder walking through that door.

What AI can't give you: the relationships, the credibility, the specific insight from years inside the problem. That lived experience is back to being the actual edge.

---

The practical implication:

If you're raising, the question isn't "what can you build?" anymore. It's "why are you the right person to sell this to these specific buyers?"

If you're bootstrapping: this is actually great news. The bar to build has dropped while the bar to raise has gone up. Bootstrap until you have proof of distribution, then negotiate from a position of strength.

The founders getting funded in 2026 aren't necessarily the best coders.

They're the ones who already know exactly who's going to buy — and have receipts.
