## Post

Privacy enforcement is no longer theoretical.

Fines are hitting real companies. The California Privacy Protection Agency has issued enforcement actions against brands that most SaaS founders assumed were "doing fine."

Here's what I've learned building a product that touches user data every day:

Compliance is not a legal checkbox. It's a product decision.

The instinct is to collect everything and figure out the data governance later. That instinct is expensive.

Every data point you store is a liability if you can't justify why you need it. The discipline of "what do we actually need?" sounds slow. But it's the thing that makes compliance cheap instead of catastrophic.

A few rules I actually follow:

Consent by default, not consent as an afterthought. If you're designing a UI and the "decline" path is harder than the "accept" path, that's a product bug — not just a legal risk.

Data minimization is a feature. Users notice when a product asks for less than expected. It builds trust in a way that marketing copy doesn't.

Build for enforcement, not just the current rules. Regulations will tighten. If you architect as if auditors are watching now, you don't have to scramble later.

The founders who treat privacy as a competitive moat — not a compliance cost — are the ones I'd bet on in the next five years.

The rules are only getting stricter.
