## Post

Raising money too early is how most startups die slowly.

Not fast. Slowly.

You get the wire. You hire faster than your revenue supports. You optimize for the metrics you promised investors, not the ones that actually matter. Then the next raise comes around — and the story doesn't hold up.

The founders winning quietly in 2026 are doing something different.

They're bootstrapping to a real number — $20k MRR, $50k MRR, $100k MRR — and then raising.

Median seed rounds sit at $3.1M right now. Coming in with traction means you negotiate the terms instead of accepting them. You're selling a share of something proven, not a bet on something hypothetical.

Two paths exist:

Path A: $0 revenue → raise → you're now optimizing for a story you promised someone else.

Path B: Build to traction → raise → capital becomes accelerant, not oxygen.

Path B is slower up front. But you walk into every investor conversation from a position of leverage.

The bar to raise has never been higher for non-AI startups — what used to get a seed done now barely gets a meeting. The founders who skip that problem are the ones who proved the business first.

Only 1% of startups ever receive venture capital. The other 99% have to figure out the business before anyone will write a check.

Turns out that constraint is the best training you'll ever get. The constraint is the point.
