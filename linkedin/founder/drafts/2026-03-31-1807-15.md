## Post

75% of VC-backed startups fail.

90% of bootstrapped startups that reach $1M in revenue are still operating 10 years later.

Nobody puts that second stat on a pitch deck.

The venture capital narrative is loud. It dominates podcasts, conferences, founder Twitter. Raise. Scale. Exit. The entire machine signals that funding equals legitimacy.

But the actual numbers tell a different story.

Bootstrapped founders hit profitability in 12–24 months. VC-backed founders often wait 5–10 years.

Bootstrapped founders retain ~70% ownership at exit. VC-backed founders, after multiple dilution rounds, often hold around 20%.

The math only works if your exit is large enough to make up for everything you gave away. Most exits aren't.

Here's what rarely gets said out loud: venture capital changes what you're optimizing for — permanently. The moment you take institutional money, you stop building toward sustainable profit and start building toward the next round. That's not cynical. It's structural. It's how the incentives are designed.

For certain companies, that trade is exactly right. You need capital to build payments infrastructure, AI training pipelines, biotech. You can't bootstrap that at scale.

But a huge number of software companies — especially SaaS — get pressured into the VC playbook before they've even confirmed product-market fit. They raise to hire. They hire to grow. They grow before the unit economics make sense. Then they raise again to buy more time.

That's not a growth strategy. It's a delay strategy.

The founders winning long-term aren't necessarily the ones who raised the most. They're the ones who knew — before the first pitch meeting — which game they were actually playing.

Which one are you building for?
